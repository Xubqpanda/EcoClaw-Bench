## Prompt

Context: For few-body system problems, a typical problem is to find the energy spectrum of a given quantum Hamiltonian. For example, let us consider the three-dimensional lattice Hamiltonian for a spin-1/2 non-relativistic nucleon. The Hamiltonian reads

 \\\[H = t\\sum\_{r,\\alpha, \\sigma} (2N^\\dagger\_{r,\\sigma}N\_{r,\\sigma} - N^\\dagger\_{r,\\sigma} N\_{r+e\_\\alpha,\\sigma} - N^\\dagger\_{r+e\_\\alpha,\\sigma} N\_{r,\\sigma}) + C\\times t \\sum\_{r} N^\\dagger\_{r,1}N^\\dagger\_{r,2}N\_{r,2}N\_{r,1}, \\\]

where \\(\\alpha = (x,y,z)\\), `\( e_\alpha \)` is the unit vector in the `\( \alpha \)`-th direction, and \\(t\\) is a coupling constant depending on the lattice spacing. The lattice model is constructed in the Fock space formulation, using nucleon annihilation and creation operators \\(n\_{r,\\alpha}\\) and \\(n^\\dagger\_{r,\\alpha}\\), where \\(r\\) is the lattice site on a periodic cubic spatial lattice with \\(L\\) sites in each direction and \\(\\alpha = 1,2,3,4\\) are the four flavors that label neutrons (\\(n\\)) and protons (\\(p\\)) with spin-half components \\(\\uparrow\\) and \\(\\downarrow\\). To specify, the nucleon field is given as

\\(N\_{r}=(n\_{r,\\uparrow},n\_{r,\\downarrow}, p\_{r,\\uparrow}, p\_{r,\\downarrow})^T \\).

To maintain the translational symmetry of the system, we employ the periodic boundary condition. The first step is to develop a notation to describe our multi-particle states. We first construct a complete basis of single particle states. Then we can define the complete set of two-particle states, which can be used to find matrix elements of the Hamiltonian. The energy spectrum is obtained by diagonalizing this matrix.
Question: (a) Define a single particle basis then write down the two-body state in the spin singlet channel using second quantization. The two particles are spin-1/2 nucleons. Write them down explicitly in terms of the neutron and proton basis.

(b) \(t\) is an unknown parameter of the lattice Hamiltonian, we can fix \(t\) by requiring that the lowest energy of a single particle (free particle) calculated from the lattice theory matches the lowest energy of a particle of mass \(M_{\rm phy}\) in a finite periodic physical box of length \(L_{\rm phy}\) using natural units, where \(\hbar = c=1\). (c) This a numerical question. We want to study the physics of two neutron inside a box of length \(L_{\rm phy}=3.4\) fm. In this case, if the nucleon mass is approximately 1634 MeV. What is the lowest non-zero single-particle energy?

(d) Following part (c), for the two-particle system in the spin singlet channel, let us consider the thermodynamic limit for simplicity, i.e., \(L\to\infty\) or the lattice spacing vanishes. Find the value of the two-body coupling constant \(C \) such that the two-body binding energy is -17.8 MeV. What is the energy of the first excited state?

(e) Use the theory to calculate the energy of the second excited state and what is the degeneracy of this state?
